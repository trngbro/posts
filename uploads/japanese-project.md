## When Code Stops Feeling Like Code

Working on Japanese client projects gives you a very unique experience.  
You open a source file expecting familiar syntax, but instead you walk straight into what feels like a language exam. The language is VB.NET, yes, but everything meaningful around it is in Japanese: variable names, comments, conditional logic explanations, and even subtle business rules hidden behind Kanji.

---

## Debugging or Language Training?

At some point you stop asking *“What does this function do?”*  
Instead, you start asking *“What does this sentence even mean?”*

To actually survive, you need three things:

- You must know how to code
- You must understand the business logic
- You must be able to read Japanese

Miss any one of those, and the whole thing becomes a puzzle that refuses to make sense.

---

## Irony in the Best and Worst Way

Here’s the funniest part: everything is technically great.  
The naming is meaningful.  
The documentation is detailed.  
The structure is strict and disciplined.

It is genuinely well engineered… just entirely locked behind another language. Meanwhile, non-Japanese developers gain an unexpected “bonus feature”: a free Japanese reading course, generously packaged with stress and occasional existential questioning.

---

### Final Thought

Japanese projects are impressive, frustrating, challenging, and oddly memorable. You don’t just write software there — you earn the ability to read Kanji under pressure. Whether that’s a feature or a bug is still up for debate.
